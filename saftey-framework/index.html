Safety Levels

A framework for classifying how effectively safety has been designed into a device.

How safe is a device, really? And how much does it depend on the person using it? This framework builds on the Hierarchy of Controls to give a clear answer. Think of it like IP ratings for safety: a simple number that tells you what you're dealing with.

Take the Petzl GriGri, a belay device with a cam that pinches the rope when a climber falls. Many assume it will lock automatically, but catastrophic failures can occur when operators believe its safety level is higher than it is.

0

No Protection

The device offers no protective features or guidance. The hazard is fully exposed.

Examples
  • –A vintage bench grinder with no wheel guards, no eye shields, and no spark arrestors. The spinning abrasive wheel is fully exposed during operation.
  • –An open fire pit with no screen or barrier. Anyone nearby can fall in or be burned by sparks.
1

Safe Only With Correct Use

The device provides no engineering controls to help the operator. Safety relies entirely on user skill, procedures and PPE.

Examples
  • –A basic chainsaw where safety depends on operator technique, stance, and PPE (chaps, helmet, gloves). The device itself offers little protection; training and discipline are the primary safeguards.
  • –A sharp kitchen knife. Proper grip and cutting technique are essential; the knife does nothing to prevent cuts.
2

Safety Encouraged

The device provides cues, markings or warnings to encourage correct behaviour, but does not block unsafe use.

Examples
  • –An extension ladder with angle indicators, "this side up" labels, and anti-slip feet. These cues guide correct setup, but nothing prevents the operator from ignoring them.
  • –A circular saw with blade guard markings and a safety sticker. The warnings are visible, but nothing prevents the operator from removing the guard.
3

Safety Assisted

The device has built-in features that reduce risk automatically, but incorrect technique or accidental misuse can still result in harm.

Examples
  • –A power drill with electronic brake, torque limiter, and insulated casing. These features reduce injury risk, but the operator can still slip or drill into something they shouldn't.
  • –A car with seatbelts and airbags. The safety systems help significantly in a crash, but reckless driving can still cause serious harm.
4

Fail-Safe

The device forces a safe state that cannot be overridden by the operator. No amount of incorrect technique, inattention, or misuse can bypass the protection during normal operation.

Examples
  • –A microwave oven with redundant door interlocks. The magnetron physically cannot energise unless all switches confirm the door is sealed. The operator cannot run the microwave with the door open.
  • –A lift with door interlocks and slack-rope brakes. The car cannot move unless the doors are closed, and it will stop automatically if the cable fails.
5

Inherently Safe

The danger is designed out entirely. The device simply cannot cause serious harm through normal use.

Examples
  • –A plastic letter opener instead of a metal blade. It can open envelopes but cannot cut skin.
  • –A battery-powered desk fan instead of a mains-powered one. The low voltage cannot deliver a dangerous shock.
