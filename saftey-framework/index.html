Safety Levels

A framework for reasoning about device safety design.

How safe is a device, really? And how much does it depend on the person using it? This framework builds on the Hierarchy of Controls to give a clear answer. Think of it like IP ratings for safety: a simple number that tells you what you're dealing with.

Take the Petzl GriGri, a belay device with a cam that pinches the rope when a climber falls. Many assume it will lock automatically, but catastrophic failures occur when operators believe its safety level is higher than it is.

0

No Protection

Hazard fully exposed; no protective features or guidance.

Example

A vintage bench grinder with no wheel guards, no eye shields, and no spark arrestors. The spinning abrasive wheel is fully exposed during operation.

1

Safe Only With Correct Use

Safety relies entirely on user skill, procedures and PPE. The device provides no engineering controls to help the operator.

Example

A basic chainsaw where safety depends almost entirely on operator technique, stance, and PPE (chaps, helmet, gloves). The device itself offers little protection; training and discipline are the primary safeguards.

2

Safety Encouraged

The device provides cues, markings or warnings to encourage correct behaviour, but does not block unsafe use.

Example

An extension ladder with angle indicators, "this side up" labels, and anti-slip feet. These cues guide correct setup, but nothing prevents the operator from ignoring them or climbing unsafely.

3

Safety Assisted

Built-in design features reduce risk automatically under normal conditions, but incorrect technique or accidental misuse can still result in harm.

Example

A power drill with electronic brake, torque limiter, and insulated casing. These features reduce injury risk during normal use, but the operator can still defeat them through misuse or incorrect technique.

4

Fail-Safe

The system forces a safe state that cannot be defeated by operator behaviour. No amount of incorrect technique, inattention, or misuse can override the protection during normal operation.

Example

A microwave oven with redundant door interlocks. The magnetron physically cannot energise unless all switches confirm the door is sealed; opening the door instantly cuts power. The operator cannot choose to run the microwave with the door open. The interlock makes operator misuse impossible under normal conditions.

5

Inherently Safe

The hazardous energy or condition is designed out or substituted so that normal use cannot realistically create serious harm.

Example

Substituting a rotary paper trimmer with a safety blade that retracts into a guard after each cut, or replacing a solvent-based process with a water-based alternative. The hazard itself is eliminated or reduced to a level where serious harm is not realistically possible under normal use.
